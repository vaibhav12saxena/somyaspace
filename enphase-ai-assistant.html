Problem

Customers were waiting for a human to answer basic questions

Enphase customers, homeowners and installers alike, were waiting for a human to answer basic questions about their solar system. Simple queries about energy control, installation diagnostics, or fleet analytics led to long call queues, outdated knowledge bases, and manual ticket triaging.

Support teams were overwhelmed and customers couldn't self-serve. The gap between what people needed to know and how fast they could get answers kept growing with every product update and new hardware release.

Solution

One AI layer, three personas, four ingress points

One unified AI layer, powered by Claude, Gemini, and 70+ specialized agents, serves three distinct personas across four product surfaces. The AI adapts its tone, depth, and response format based on who's asking.

Homeowner
Non-technical users who own solar + battery systems. They want to understand their energy data, manage costs, and troubleshoot without calling support.
AI tone: Simple, empathetic, jargon-free
Context: System status, billing, energy usage
Installer
Certified solar professionals who install, commission, and maintain Enphase systems. On-site, time-pressured, technically fluent. Need fast, precise answers.
AI tone: Direct, technical, no-fluff
Context: Device telemetry, site config, diagnostics
Support Agent
Internal CS reps handling inbound tickets. They need instant access to site data, troubleshooting paths, and knowledge base articles to resolve issues faster.
AI tone: Structured, context-rich, actionable
Context: Ticket history, fleet data, KB articles

AI Design Guidelines

Grounding the experience in proven AI principles

Designing an AI assistant for something as technical as solar energy meant we couldn't wing it. I used Microsoft's 18 AI Design Guidelines as a framework to structure our design decisions, adapting each principle to Enphase's context of homeowners, installers, and support agents.

These guidelines helped me work through a real tension: the AI needed to feel smart and capable while being upfront about its limits. Here's how I mapped the most critical principles to our design:

Key principles applied

Out of 18 guidelines, these 7 shaped the most critical design decisions.

01
Graceful escalation when AI can't help
Recognize when the assistant doesn't understand the user's ask or lacks the confidence to answer accurately.
Applied: When the assistant detects low confidence or repeated misunderstanding, it proactively suggests connecting with an Enphase support agent instead of guessing.
02
Transparent thinking and loading states
Show users what's happening in the system while the AI processes their request.
Applied: Multi-stage indicators ("Analyzing your image...", "Fetching system data...", "Preparing insights...") keep users informed instead of staring at a spinner. Each query type has its own sequence of thinking states tailored to what's actually happening behind the scenes.
AI thinking steps
Handoff loading states
03
Context-aware preset menus
Surface relevant quick-action options based on the user's persona and current context, so they don't have to type from scratch.
Applied: Homeowners see prompts like "Why is my bill high?" and "Check battery status." Installers see "Run commissioning check" and "Gateway LED troubleshooting." Menus adapt to active alerts and recent activity.
04
Make clear what the system can do
Help the user understand what the AI is capable of during onboarding and throughout interactions.
Applied: The assistant takes context from where it's invoked. In the Homeowner App, it opens with energy-specific prompts. In ITK, it surfaces installer workflows. On the website, it asks who you are first, then tailors the experience.
06
Equitable, persona-adaptive tone
Adapt language per persona without assumptions about technical literacy.
Applied: Tone and vocabulary adjust per persona (homeowner vs. installer) while keeping the same level of respect and clarity for both.
05
Non-blocking AI responses
Let users continue using the app while the AI generates its response, so they're never stuck waiting.
Applied: The chat runs as an overlay. Users can navigate the app, check system data, or review alerts while the assistant works in the background.
07
Take feedback and give fallback contextual menu options
When the assistant can't fully resolve a query, offer contextual fallback options instead of a dead end, and always let users share feedback on the response quality.
Applied: Every response includes a feedback mechanism. When the assistant reaches its limits, it surfaces relevant menu options like contacting support, browsing help articles, or restarting the conversation, so users always have a next step.
Why this mattered: With an AI assistant spanning homeowner apps, installer tools, support dashboards, and the public website, the number of use cases grew fast. A structured framework meant we weren't redesigning from scratch for every new context. These guidelines gave us a scalable foundation, consistent principles that held up across systems while still flexing for each persona and platform.
Design in Action

Visual design principles

The assistant lives across multiple platforms, so the visual language had to feel native everywhere while still being recognizable as the AI assistant.

●
Yellow as the AI identity
The yellow accent color is the consistent thread across every platform. Wherever users encounter the AI assistant, whether in the homeowner app, installer toolkit, or main website, yellow signals "this is the AI." It creates instant recognition without needing a label.
🎨
Tappable text follows native app color
CTA buttons use the AI's yellow identity to stay visually consistent across every platform. But clickable text inside the chat follows the native color of the host app, blue in the homeowner app, orange in installer tools, yellow on the website. Users have already built muscle memory for what looks tappable in each app. Overriding that with a new color would break learned affordances and force them to relearn interaction cues inside the assistant. The split keeps the AI recognizable while respecting each platform's existing visual language.
🔄
Icon swap on agent handoff
When the AI assistant transfers the conversation to a human support agent, two things change at once. The assistant icon swaps to an agent icon, and the chat theme shifts from yellow to the native app color. This double signal, visual identity plus color environment, makes the transition unmistakable. Users don't need to read a system message to know they're now talking to a real person. The color shift leverages a principle from cognitive psychology: environmental context change signals a mode switch, reducing the chance of users treating agent responses as AI generated.

Solving for ambiguous and complicated systems

The Enphase ecosystem is not a single product. It spans homeowner apps, installer tools, fleet management dashboards, support portals, and the public website. Each surface has different users, different data access, and different levels of technical complexity. A homeowner asking "why is my bill high?" and an installer debugging a gateway communication fault are fundamentally different problems, but both land in the same AI assistant.

How the assistant handles complexity

We mapped four foundational patterns to cover how the assistant operates across different levels of system complexity and user intent.

{}
Fetch and display via public APIs
The assistant taps into Enphase's publicly accessible APIs to pull and surface real-time data like system status, production and consumption metrics, device health, and EV charge control, directly inside the chat.
Outcome: Users get their system information instantly without navigating complex menus or dashboards.
🖥
Task automation on behalf of the user
For actions like restarting a device, re-triggering a data sync, or checking support case statuses, the assistant triggers backend workflows after user confirmation, rather than just telling them what to do.
Outcome: Fewer manual steps, faster resolution, less room for user error.
🔒
Restricted access and secure redirection
For sensitive actions like billing queries and warranty claims that require higher authentication or portal access, the assistant provides controlled guidance, presenting secure links and redirecting users safely to the right section.
Outcome: Data security maintained while still completing complex workflows end to end.
🪜
Step-by-step guided troubleshooting
For multi-step tasks like troubleshooting communication failures or reconnecting to Wi-Fi, the assistant breaks the process into clear, sequential steps with the ability to ask for clarification or repeat any step.
Outcome: Users self-resolve issues without feeling overwhelmed by technical complexity.
Design Iterations

Shipped, learned, iterated, repeat

The AI assistant wasn't designed once and shipped. It evolved through continuous iteration cycles, each informed by real user behaviour, beta metrics, and direct feedback from homeowners and installers in the pilot.

Iteration 01
Salesforce shell, if/else logic, website only
The first version launched in early 2024 as a pilot scoped to the Enphase website only. The backend ran on if/else branching logic rather than a true LLM, which made conversations feel rigid and IVR like. The UI followed the Salesforce design system because CS agents were already using a Salesforce chatbot to handle customer chats, and reusing it meant faster developer implementation. As we added features, the system's limitations became clear. Branching logic couldn't handle ambiguous queries, the Salesforce shell restricted layout flexibility, and the whole experience felt more like a decision tree than a conversation.
Iteration 02
Built from scratch, scalable across platforms
The V1 limitations made it clear we needed a ground up rebuild. This version was designed to scale across the homeowner app, installer tools, Enlighten Manager, and the website. When launched from the homeowner or installer apps, the assistant automatically picks up the user's persona and system context. On the website, it asks. New features like conversation threads, chat and case history, and a fixed menu bar were introduced to help users navigate complex data without losing track of where they were. A lead capture flow was also built for guest visitors on the website, turning support interactions into new business opportunities.
Alpha Feedback
Alpha feedback and friction fixes
The overall design direction and LLM response quality received decent feedback during alpha testing. But two specific friction points stood out.
Preset menu wasn't discoverable
Users kept asking: "How do I see the menu options that were shown to me when I opened the interface? Should I exit the assistant every time and open a fresh chat to see those options?" The preset menu only appeared on first launch and disappeared once a conversation started. We moved it to a persistent, always accessible location.

Feedback and utility actions hidden behind overflow
Actions like response feedback (thumbs up/down), read aloud, and copy were tucked behind a three dot overflow menu on each response. Usage was low. Since this was a new product launch and we needed feedback data to train and improve the models, burying the feedback controls was working against us. We surfaced them directly under every response.

Beta Tracking
Threads bar removed, new chat relocated
During beta tracking with 229K+ users, we noticed that the threads generated at the top of the interface were barely being used. Most users continued their conversations one after another in the same chat rather than starting new threads. The data confirmed this: fewer than 8% of sessions used the "New chat" button in the top bar, and thread switching accounted for less than 3% of all interactions. Users were in a hurry to resolve issues, not organize their chat history. The threads bar was taking up valuable screen real estate without adding proportional value. We removed it from the top and relocated the new chat action into the fixed menu bar at the bottom, freeing up vertical space for what mattered most: the conversation itself.
